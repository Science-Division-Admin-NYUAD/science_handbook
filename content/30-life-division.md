### Course Preparation

#### Course Preparation Checklist
Faculty must complete the following course preparation items in order to adequately prepare to begin teaching a course at NYU Abu Dhabi. Before commencing course preparation, faculty should contact their Program Head or Director of the Core to receive guidance on developing a course description.

1. Create a syllabus and submit it for approval (new courses or significantly revised courses).
2. Make approved syllabi (for new courses) and updated syllabi (for recurring courses) available on the Intranet for students and other university members.
3. Order course books.
4. Order non-text materials (email the NYUAD Bookstore).
5. Activate your NYU Brightspace course site.
6. Apply for domestic course-related funding.
7. Submit your software list (email nyuad.academictech@nyu.edu).
8. Request other Library services and learn about the Library Liaison Program.
9. Upload all required materials to the Course Archive and Reporting (CAaR) system.

#### Syllabi Submission
NYUAD requires that current syllabi be uploaded for all courses as early as possible and certainly before the start of the term. Updated syllabi for existing courses without any substantive changes can be submitted directly to the web for posting.

Syllabi for new courses or for existing courses with substantive changes need to be reviewed prior to being posted online. Substantive changes ordinarily include, but are not limited to: changes in the course learning objectives, changes in prerequisites, significant changes in the types and/or weighting of graded assignments, and substantive changes to the course description. The review process for new or substantively revised courses begins with the Program Head, followed by the Divisional Dean (or Director of the Core), the Divisional Curricular Committee, and then, finally, the Undergraduate or Graduate Curriculum Committee (UCC or GGCC).

Before submitting your syllabus, please read the Syllabi guidelines and review the Syllabi Checklist (PDF) to ensure that syllabi are complete.

#### Course Book Ordering
Submitting syllabi is a different and separate procedure from ordering course books. Orders for required course books must be submitted directly using the online Course Book Ordering.

Some things to keep in mind:

- Ordering course books through the NYUAD Bookstore does not ensure a copy of each title will be made available in the library. Please submit syllabi to the library to ensure supporting texts are available.
- You can order additional books at any time by following the same guidelines.
- Required items will be issued to each student enrolled in the class.
- Recommended items will be available to students to purchase as desired. Three to five copies will be ordered for each class.
- Status and Expected Delivery will be regularly updated by the NYUAD Bookstore.
- Other (non-text) course material can be ordered by emailing nyuad.bookstore@nyu.edu, detailing the item you need (description, make/model and quantities).

## Teaching & Learning Resources

#### The Office of Undergraduate Education
The Office of Undergraduate Education focuses on undergraduate teaching, learning, and academic mentoring. Areas that fall within Undergraduate Education include the Core Curriculum, John Sexton and Lisa Goldberg Academic Resource Center, Writing Center, Hilary Ballon Center for Excellence in Teaching and Learning, Registrar, Academic Policies, and the Office of Student Research.

#### The Hilary Ballon Center for Excellence in Teaching and Learning
Offers individual consultations and a variety of workshops to support your teaching and learning needs. It exists to support faculty in bringing the mission and vision of the University to life in the classroom and provides materials, ideas, and a community to support extraordinary teaching.

#### John Sexton and Lisa Goldberg Academic Resource Center (ARC)
Provides students with resources and support to help them achieve their academic goals. Academic Advisors utilize a student-centered approach and developmental advisory strategies to help new and continuing students succeed through to graduation.

#### Writing Program
Courses offered by the NYUAD Writing Program are opportunities to concentrate intensely on the process of writing. The Program champions an integrated understanding of writing as a mode of inquiry and invention; as a means of communicating the results of that inquiry; and as a social and recursive activity.

#### The Office of Undergraduate Research
Research is an important part of education at NYUAD and research opportunities are threaded throughout the undergraduate program across all four academic divisions. Students become active investigators and experience the challenge, creativity, and rigor involved in grappling with unanswered questions.

#### Faculty Advancement and Engagement
The Office for Faculty Advancement and Engagement develops and implements strategies and programs so that all faculty can excel as scholars, teachers, and impactful members of the academic community.

### Research: Space and Safety

![Research space and safety](assets/images/scene-p38.jpg){.hero-img}

#### Space Access Requests
Space access requests should be submitted to Public Safety. Requests will be approved based on the expectation that a person has already, or will as soon as possible, complete all OSHMS and EHS training requirements. Non-compliance will result in access being revoked.

Requests for undergraduate student access must be completed by the PI or supervising research staff member. Please leave a comment in the request form that details which year of study they are in, confirm they have taken the Laboratory Safety Induction training, and what supervision plans you have in place.

Undergraduate students: All undergraduate students requesting card access to laboratories, workshops or equipment rooms must have completed their Laboratory Safety Induction training prior to access being granted. First- and second-year undergraduates will not be granted card access to research laboratories outside of normal operational hours (7am–6pm / Mon–Fri) and should never be permitted to work unsupervised. Third- and fourth-year undergraduates may be granted out-of-hours access on a case-by-case basis as determined by the respective Dean.

::: note
NOTE: Students should never work alone in laboratories, workshops or equipment rooms, and 'hazardous' operations/experiments should only be performed in the presence of a trained laboratory staff member or technician.
:::

#### Environmental Health and Safety (EHS)
All personnel in the Division of Science must read and implement the OSH Policy. EHS provides training via an online course management system called Moodle, supplemented with additional online and classroom training, depending on the risks involved in your area of work.

Irrespective of role or work area, it is mandatory that all personnel in the Division of Science complete the following three trainings on Moodle:

1. All nine elements of the NYUAD OSH Management System.
2. The online NYUAD EHS Induction training.
3. The online Fire Safety Induction training.

In addition, if a new joiner is requesting access to the C1 (ERB) building or is expected to work in any laboratory, they must complete the EHS Classroom Training within their first 60 days. If this is not done, access will be revoked. It is the responsibility of the PI to ensure all group personnel are trained, correctly make use of Chemtracker and AssessNET, are aware of risks and SOPs, and know where and how to report.

#### See something? Report It. (EHS)
All personnel should be aware that "Report It" is the appropriate channel to report all hazards, incidents, and safety concerns (in addition to notifying your supervisor). Report It is accessible through the EHS Intranet homepage. Please immediately notify EHS and PSD (Public Safety Department) of serious incidents. Contact: nyuad.ehs@nyu.edu

## Program Seminar Series & Research Talks

![Program seminars and research talks](assets/images/scene-p40.jpg){.hero-img}

#### Program Seminar Series
Each program in the Division of Science hosts a collection of semi-formal weekly talks throughout the academic year. These Seminar Series — or Colloquium Series in the case of Mathematics — build the reputation of the programs, division, and university at large by facilitating networking connections and research collaborations with local and international scholars.

Seminars are open to the NYUAD community, and may be attended by staff, faculty, students, postdocs, and researchers. Speakers are advised that topics should not be too niche, but rather broad enough to be understood by an audience of varied specializations. Some Programs in the Division of Science also organize informal research talks, offering PhD graduates and research staff the opportunity to present their work to peers.

#### Seminar Series Planning and Policies
Your Program Administrative Coordinators will work with you to plan and schedule seminar speakers. Each standing faculty member can usually invite at least one seminar speaker per academic year. Additional speakers may be invited at the discretion of the Program Head and Administrator.

NYUAD offers a complimentary economy return ticket, airport ground transfers in the UAE, campus or local hotel accommodation (depending on availability), and a visa if required. If a speaker's flight time is more than eight hours, the program budget may cover up to four nights' accommodation; if eight hours or less, three nights. The program also covers the cost of one seminar dinner, capped at AED 300 per person up to a maximum of AED 1,200, and alcohol cannot be reimbursed. NYUAD policy will not reimburse speakers for booking their own travel.

#### Global Perspectives in Science Lecture Series
Each month, the Division of Science hosts an international speaker as part of its Global Perspectives in Science Lecture Series. The mission of the series is to expand academic networks by hosting leading scholars who are experts in scientific fields currently unexplored at NYUAD, or who come from regions or institutions with which faculty in the Division of Science have not yet collaborated. Faculty can propose speakers to Julie Brookes at julie.brookes@nyu.edu.

## Capstones

![Science Capstone Festival](assets/images/scene-p42.jpg){.hero-img}

The Capstone Project is one of the culminating experiences for all students at NYUAD. It is a mandatory requirement and completion is scheduled for the student's fourth year. The Capstone experience in the Division of Science requires students to engage in a long-term, mentored learning experience that culminates in a piece of original research, scientific theory, or both. This approach enables students to:

1. experience the teamwork required to succeed in the sciences,
2. foster a strong relationship with a faculty mentor, and
3. engage in weekly lab meetings and other research-related activities.

Students are expected to use this experience to develop a Capstone thesis that either qualifies as an empirical project or as a theoretical treatise. In both cases, the thesis consists of a written document of significant length that describes the work in detail.

#### Science Capstone Festival
Students are expected to present their work at the annual Science Capstone Festival, which is typically hosted in April. Program faculty are strongly encouraged to attend the festival, which is open to the whole community.

#### Capstone Research Seminar Courses
No matter what form the Capstone takes, each student will have a faculty mentor and participate in a Capstone seminar that serves as a collaborative learning forum to discuss the research process and present work in progress. The seminar provides students with an overview of the diverse multidisciplinary research areas that have captured the interest of NYUAD scientists. The final Capstone proposal is due at the end of the Capstone Seminar course (in the student's junior year) so that students can begin their Capstone Project in the fall semester of their senior year.
